About Me

Hi There,

My name is Thi Bich Phuong Dao and I am currently pursuing an Early Childhood Education Diploma at Humber Polytechnic. Through my studies, I have built a strong foundation in child development, play-based learning, and inclusive education. My experiences in classroom observations, lesson planning, and interactive activities have allowed me to support children's growth in meaningful ways. I have also developed skills in responsive caregiving and positive guidance to create safe and engaging learning environments. I have a deep understanding of early learning frameworks and child-centered teaching approaches. I value collaboration with families and educators to provide hands-on, developmentally appropriate experiences that nurture children's curiosity, independence, and overall well-being.

My goal is to make a lasting, positive impact by fostering a love for learning in a safe, engaging, and nurturing space.

Teaching & Learning Philosophy

Learning as a Journey

When I think about teaching and learning, I no longer imagine a straight path with clear steps and fixed outcomes. Instead, I see it as a journey that moves in different directions, sometimes slowly, sometimes unexpectedly. Through my practicum experiences, I have come to understand that learning is not something we control, but something we support. This realization has changed the way I see children, and more importantly, the way I see myself as an educator.

I believe children are naturally capable and full of ideas. They are not waiting to be taught everything. They are already thinking, questioning, and making meaning from the world around them. During my practicum, I noticed that when children were given time and freedom to explore, their engagement became deeper and more genuine. They were not just participating in an activity, they were fully involved in it. This reflects Vygotsky's idea that learning develops through social interaction and shared experiences, where children actively construct understanding rather than passively receive it (Vygotsky, 1978). Seeing this in real moments helped me trust children more and step away from the idea that I always need to direct their learning.

I believe my role as an educator is to support, not control, the learning process. At the beginning of my placement, I often felt responsible for guiding children step by step, making sure everything went as planned. However, I started to realize that when I stepped back, children became more confident in making their own decisions. They solved problems in ways I did not expect. That was the moment I understood that teaching is not about having all the answers. It is about knowing when to listen, when to observe, and when to gently support. This perspective connects with Dewey's belief that education should be grounded in meaningful experiences, where learning comes from doing rather than being instructed (Dewey, 1938). Letting go of control was not easy for me, but it was necessary for children to truly lead their own learning.

I believe families are an essential part of a child's learning journey. Every child brings their own background, culture, and experiences into the classroom. These are not separate from learning, they are the foundation of it. Through my practicum, I became more aware that building trust with families is just as important as building relationships with children. Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems theory reminds us that development is shaped by multiple layers of relationships and environments, especially the family context (Bronfenbrenner, 1979). This understanding helped me see that I am not teaching a child in isolation. I am supporting a child who is deeply connected to their family and community.

I believe the community and social environment influence how children see themselves and others. Children are constantly observing the world around them. They notice differences, relationships, and how people are treated. This has made me reflect on the importance of creating inclusive environments where every child feels seen and valued. It is not only about what we teach, but also about what children experience and how they feel in that space.

I believe the environment is not just a background, but an active part of learning. During my practicum, I saw how the arrangement of materials and space could either invite exploration or limit it. When materials were accessible and thoughtfully presented, children became more independent and engaged. This aligns with the Reggio Emilia approach, which views the environment as the "third teacher," recognizing that learning happens through interaction with space, materials, and others (Edwards, Gandini, & Forman, 2012). This perspective has changed how I think about planning. Instead of focusing only on activities, I now think about how the environment itself can support learning.

Looking back, my philosophy has grown from my own experiences of trying, adjusting, and reflecting. I am still learning how to balance guidance and freedom, structure and flexibility. I do not see teaching as reaching a final point, but as continuing to grow alongside the children. I want to remain someone who listens carefully, reflects honestly, and creates spaces where children feel safe to explore, express, and become who they are.
