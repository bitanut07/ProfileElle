About Me

Hi There,

My name is Thi Bich Phuong Dao and I am currently pursuing an Early Childhood Education Diploma at Humber Polytechnic. Through my studies, I have built a strong foundation in child development, play-based learning, and inclusive education. My experiences in classroom observations, lesson planning, and interactive activities have allowed me to support children's growth in meaningful ways. I have also developed skills in responsive caregiving and positive guidance to create safe and engaging learning environments. I have a deep understanding of early learning frameworks and child-centered teaching approaches. I value collaboration with families and educators to provide hands-on, developmentally appropriate experiences that nurture children's curiosity, independence, and overall well-being.

Looking forward, I aim to create inclusive and supportive learning environments where all children feel valued and encouraged to explore. I am particularly passionate about integrating arts-based learning and creative expression into early education, using storytelling, music, and interactive play to enhance children's development. My goal is to make a lasting, positive impact by fostering a love for learning in a safe, engaging, and nurturing space.

Teaching & Learning Philosophy

Learning as a Journey

I believe teaching and learning are not a straight path, but an ongoing journey shaped by reflection, relationships, and experience. Children are capable, curious learners who build knowledge through exploration, play, and social interaction (Vygotsky, 1978). My role as an educator is to support rather than control learning by observing carefully, listening deeply, and offering guidance when needed (Dewey, 1938). I also believe families and communities are essential to children’s development because learning is deeply connected to relationships, culture, and lived experiences (Bronfenbrenner, 1979). The environment is another important teacher, as accessible materials and thoughtful spaces can invite independence, creativity, and engagement (Edwards et al., 2012). Overall, my philosophy is grounded in creating inclusive, responsive spaces where children feel safe, valued, and free to become themselves.

2. Learning Story

Name of Child: Giang

Date of Birth: January 2nd, 2019

Location: In play area

Date of Observation: March 5th, 2025

Observer: Thi Bich Phuong Dao

Developmental Area: Social & Emotional Development

Today, I observed Giang fully immersed in her role as a doctor. Dressed in a pink doctor's coat and hat, she moved confidently around the play area, her eyes gleaming with excitement. She carefully arranged her medical tools on the table, inspecting each one before beginning her "treatments."

With gentle hands, she placed a toy stethoscope on a stuffed animal and whispered reassuringly, "Don't worry, I'm here to help." Her expression was full of kindness and determination as she tended to each patient, demonstrating an impressive level of care and empathy. When a friend mentioned having a pretend injury, Giang quickly offered a toy bandage, her nurturing instincts shining through.

Reflecting and Interpreting the Learning That Took Place

Giang's imaginative play highlights her developing social and emotional skills. She showed deep empathy and problem-solving abilities, thoughtfully responding to the needs of her "patients." Her ability to engage in role-play with props demonstrates her understanding of real-world professions and responsibilities.

Through these interactions, Giang displayed confidence in taking on leadership roles, showing a strong sense of responsibility. She also exhibited cooperative play, willingly assisting her peers and ensuring they felt included in the activity.

Future Opportunities and Extension of Learning

To further nurture Giang's interest in role-playing, introducing additional costumes and props from various professions — such as firefighters, chefs, or teachers — can expand her imagination and understanding of different roles. Reading books about doctors and hospitals could help enrich her knowledge and vocabulary.

Setting up a more structured "clinic" area with dolls and interactive doctor tools can provide new opportunities for learning. Encouraging her to verbalize her actions and thoughts while playing will further develop her communication skills.

As an Early Childhood Educator, I will continue to support Giang's social and emotional growth by providing engaging role-play opportunities. Through these experiences, she will continue to build empathy, problem-solving skills, and teamwork, all while having fun in her imaginative world.

5. Resources

Resource 1: Ontario's Pedagogy for the Early Years

(How Does Learning Happen?, Excerpts from ELECT, and Think, Feel, Act)

This visual representation illustrates key ideas within Ontario's Pedagogy for the Early Years, including belonging, well-being, engagement, and expression.

I chose Ontario's Pedagogy for the Early Years because it has influenced the way I think about children, learning, and my role as an educator more than any other resource. Together, How Does Learning Happen?, Excerpts from ELECT, and Think, Feel, Act have helped me move away from seeing teaching as simply planning activities and encouraged me to think more deeply about relationships, belonging, engagement, and expression. In my practice, I have used these resources to guide the way I observe children, reflect on their learning, and make more intentional decisions about how to respond. I can see their impact in the artifacts I included in this portfolio, especially in the way I value children's curiosity during sensory exploration and in how I reflect on my own growth through mentor feedback. I included this resource because it closely reflects my belief that children are capable, curious, and full of ideas, and that meaningful learning happens through relationships, play, and thoughtful practice.

Resource 2: ABC Life Literacy Canada – Family Literacy First

I chose Family Literacy First because it helped me rethink what literacy can look like outside of the classroom. Before, I often connected literacy with reading books, but this resource reminded me that it can also grow through simple, shared moments between children and their families. In my practice, this way of thinking influenced how I approached communication with families, especially in finding ways to make learning feel more natural and connected to their everyday lives rather than something formal or separate. It also made me more aware of how important home language, culture, and family experiences are in shaping a child’s understanding of the world. I included this resource because it reflects how I want to support families in ways that feel realistic, respectful, and meaningful, while recognizing that learning continues beyond the classroom (ABC Life Literacy Canada, 2022).

Resource 3: NAEYC – Developmentally Appropriate Practice (DAP)

Children learning through hands-on exploration and play-based experiences in a responsive environment.

I chose NAEYC’s Developmentally Appropriate Practice because it gave me a clearer understanding of what it means to support children’s learning without over-directing it. What stayed with me the most is the idea that learning should be responsive, hands-on, and connected to children’s interests, rather than focused on achieving a specific outcome. In my practice, this has influenced how I think about my role during play, especially learning to step back, observe more closely, and trust children’s ability to explore and make sense of their experiences. Instead of feeling the need to guide every moment, I am learning to create space for children to experiment, notice, and stay engaged in their own way. I included this resource because it reflects the kind of educator I am becoming — one who values curiosity, respects the learning process, and supports children in ways that feel thoughtful and intentional (NAEYC, n.d.).

Continuous Professional Learning

1. Children

Goal:

In order to better understand and support children's thinking, I will intentionally use at least two open-ended questions during play each day, while paying close attention to how children respond, throughout my first three months in practice.

Strategies:

  • I will keep a small note on my phone or notebook of questions that feel natural to me so I can use them in the moment without interrupting the flow of play.
  • At the end of each week, I will reflect on which questions actually sparked conversation or thinking, and which ones felt forced, so I can adjust my approach.

2. Families

Goal:

In order to build genuine and trusting relationships with families, I will make a consistent effort to connect with each family at least once a week through small, meaningful interactions or updates during my first three months.

Strategies:

  • I will share short, real moments from the child's day (not just general updates) so families can feel more connected to their child's experiences.
  • I will take time to listen to families' perspectives and slowly incorporate what I learn about their child into my daily interactions.

3. Colleagues

Goal:

In order to grow more confident in my role and learn from others, I will initiate at least one reflective conversation each week with a colleague about what I am noticing in the classroom during my first three months.

Strategies:

  • I will be honest about what I am unsure about and ask for feedback instead of trying to figure everything out on my own.
  • I will also share my own observations and ideas, even if they are not fully formed, to build more open and collaborative communication.

4. Communities

Goal:

In order to help children feel more connected to the world around them, I will introduce at least one element from the community (such as materials, stories, or experiences) each month that reflects their lives and backgrounds during my first three months.

Strategies:

  • I will pay attention to what children talk about (places, people, experiences) and use that as a starting point to bring the community into the classroom.
  • I will explore simple ways to include diverse and meaningful materials that reflect different cultures and experiences in an authentic way.

5. Environment

Goal:

In order to create a space that truly reflects children's interests, I will intentionally make small changes to the environment each week based on what I observe in their play during my first three months.

Strategies:

  • I will focus on observing before changing anything, so that my decisions are based on what children are actually interested in, not what I assume.
  • After making changes, I will watch how children respond and use that to decide whether to keep, adjust, or remove materials.
